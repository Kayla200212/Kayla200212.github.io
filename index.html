Project Overview

This project uses multiple grocery store datasets as well as census data for the city of Chicago to analyze food accessibility and inequality by zipcode. Some of the questions we hoped to answer were:

  • Is there a direct correlation between impoverished areas and a lower number of grocery stores, and vice versa?
  • Do an increased number of dollar stores indicate impoverished areas?
  • Do an increased number of luxury grocery stores indicate affluent areas?

First Dataset: Grocery Stores in Chicago 2013

Our first data set is one that provides a list of all grocery stores in Chicago from 2013. This dataset comes from the City of Chicagos business license data. Unfortunately, this dataset is slightly outdated (ex: one grocery store that appears in the dataset is Dominicks, a store that went out of business), but it was the only publically available dataset with a comprehensive list of stores. We also noticed that some of the grocery stores included in the list are convenience stores such as Walgreens. While Walgreens does technically have groceries, its not a fully stocked grocery store so we decided to filter it out of the dataset. Likewise, there were a number of liquor stores that technically contain food, but would not be considered a store with considerable food options. Though there are likely stores that do not get removed based on these two filters that should be, with the size of the dataset, this was the most reasonable option. Choices such as these can help further guide our ETL steps as we gather and combine more datasets. The two most important variables in this data set are the name of the grocery store and the neighborhood the grocery store is in. One question we can ask using these metrics: which neighborhoods have the least amount of grocery stores? By using this as a starting point, we can use this to begin analysis of food inequality through comparison with other datasets.

Second Dataset and Merging

This leads us into our next dataset, which provides the per-capita income of each neighborhood in Chicago.

This dataset uses six socioeconomic indicators such as per-capita income and percent of households in the neighborhood below poverty. This data comes from the Census and uses the same Community Area Name variable as the grocery store dataset, which will allow us to easily merge the datasets. We can use both of these datasets in conjunction to answer another question: what is the link between grocery store accessibility and socioeconomic markers of inequality?

Initial Findings

From our initial visualizations, the connection is vague. We visualized how the number of grocery stores per community area related to the hardship index and per capita income, respectively, and both had slight positive correlations which would indicate that an increase in stores meant both higher hardship index and higher income, but barely.

At this stage, we were encouraged to find a more recent dataset with stores we were familiar with, that we could know for certain were grocery stores or dollar stores. At present, most of the communities have less than 6 stores in them, with a few skewing towards having more than 15. More data on grocery stores in these areas would create more validity to our findings. Secondly, if we are able to classify the price range of each grocery store, this would also help us understand these results on a deeper level. Even if an area has a large amount of stores, they could be dollar stores or stores comparable to Walgreens. Knowing the price range of the stores included in the survey would also allow us to better understand the data and results.

Third Dataset

We wanted to do a comparison of "luxury" grocery stores to dollar stores, as an increase in dollar stores are often an indicator of food insecure neighborhoods (https://anderson-review.ucla.edu/how-dollar-stores-contribute-to-food-deserts/). However, there were no free datasets of these stores in Chicago. So, we used webscraping to find locations by zipcode of Whole Foods, Marianos (luxury), Dollar Trees, and Family Dollars.

  lat, lon = map(float, location.strip("()").split(','))
  return lat, lon
