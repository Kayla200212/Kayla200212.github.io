First Dataset: Grocery Stores in Chicago 2013¶

Our first data set is one that provides a list of all grocery stores in Chicago from 2013. This dataset comes from the City of Chicago’s business license data. Since this dataset is slightly outdated (one grocery store that appears in the dataset is Dominick’s, a store that went out of business), we would like to see if there is a way for us to filter or clean the business license data to get the most up to date list of grocery stores. We also noticed that some of the “grocery stores” included in the list are convenience stores such as Walgreens. While Walgreens does technically have groceries, it’s not a fully stocked grocery store so we decided to filter it out of the dataset. We would like to take a closer look at the stores included in this dataset in particular, as there might be other stores similar to Walgreens we would like to exclude. Choices such as these can help further guide our ETL steps as we gather and combine more datasets. The two most important variables in this data set are the name of the grocery store and the neighborhood the grocery store is in. One question we can ask using these metrics: which neighborhoods have the least amount of grocery stores? By using this as a starting point, we can use this to begin analysis of food inequality through comparison with other datasets.

Second Dataset and Merging¶

This leads us into our next dataset, which provides the per-capita income of each neighborhood in Chicago.

This dataset uses six socioeconomic indicators such as per-capita income and percent of households in the neighborhood below poverty. This data comes from the Census and uses the same “Community Area Name” variable as the grocery store dataset, which will allow us to easily merge the datasets. We can use both of these datasets in conjunction to answer another question: what is the link between grocery store accessibility and socioeconomic markers of inequality?

Initial Findings & Future Work¶

From our initial visualizations, the connection is vague. We visualized how the number of grocery stores per community area related to the hardship index and percentage of households below poverty, respectively, and both had slight positive correlations which would indicate that an increase in stores meant an increase in poverty indicators. This was not what we were expecting, but two things could improve the certainty of this result.

For one, as above, creating a more recent and more full dataset of stores would help greatly. At present, most of the communities have less than 6 stores in them, with a few skewing towards having more than 15. More data on grocery stores in these areas would create more validity to our findings. Secondly, if we are able to classify the price range of each grocery store, this would also help us understand these results on a deeper level. Even if an area has a large amount of stores, they could be dollar stores or stores comparable to Walgreens. Knowing the price range of the stores included in the survey would also allow us to better understand the data and results.
